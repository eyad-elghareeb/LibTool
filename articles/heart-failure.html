Heart Failure

A clinical syndrome resulting from structural or functional cardiac disorders impairing ventricular filling or ejection.

Overview & Definition

Heart failure (HF) is a complex clinical syndrome resulting from structural or functional impairment of ventricular filling or ejection of blood. It is a progressive condition characterized by dyspnea, fatigue, and fluid retention. HF represents the common final pathway of many cardiovascular diseases and carries substantial morbidity and mortality.

High-Yield HF is not a single disease but a syndrome. The hallmark is the heart's inability to pump blood at a rate commensurate with the requirements of metabolizing tissues, or doing so only from elevated filling pressures.

Classification

Heart failure is commonly classified based on left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF), which guides treatment decisions and prognosis:

  • HFrEF (Reduced EF): LVEF ≤ 40%. Also known as systolic heart failure. Most evidence-based therapies target this subtype.
  • HFmrEF (Mildly Reduced EF): LVEF 41–49%. A relatively newer classification; evidence for treatment is evolving.
  • HFpEF (Preserved EF): LVEF ≥ 50%. Also known as diastolic heart failure. Evidence of structural heart disease or diastolic dysfunction is required. Treatment is largely symptom-based.

Pathophysiology

The initial event is usually a decline in pumping capacity (e.g., myocardial infarction, valvular disease, hypertension). This triggers compensatory neurohormonal mechanisms that initially maintain cardiac output but ultimately become maladaptive, driving progressive myocardial remodeling and worsening function.

  • Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS): Increases heart rate and contractility acutely, but chronically leads to myocyte apoptosis, beta-receptor down-regulation, and increased arrhythmia risk.
  • Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS): Causes vasoconstriction and sodium/water retention, increasing preload and afterload. Promotes cardiac fibrosis and hypertrophy.
  • Counter-regulatory system: Natriuretic peptides (BNP, NT-proBNP) are released in response to ventricular stretch, promoting vasodilation and natriuresis — these serve as both biomarkers and therapeutic targets.
Clinical Warning Chronic activation of RAAS and SNS is fundamentally maladaptive and drives progressive myocardial remodeling. Pharmacological blockade of these systems forms the cornerstone of HFrEF therapy. The transition from compensation to decompensation is a key clinical concept.

Management (HFrEF)

The goals of therapy are to reduce mortality, prevent hospitalizations, and improve functional status and quality of life. The foundation is Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy (GDMT), which has been shown to significantly improve survival in clinical trials.

The Four Pillars of GDMT

  • ARNI (Sacubitril/Valsartan) or ACEi/ARB: First-line. ARNI (Entresto) is preferred over ACEi/ARB for eligible patients. Reduces mortality and hospitalizations.
  • Beta-Blockers (Carvedilol, Metoprolol succinate, Bisoprolol): Reverses ventricular remodeling. Start low, go slow. Avoid in acute decompensation.
  • MRAs (Spironolactone, Eplerenone): Aldosterone antagonism reduces fibrosis and mortality. Monitor potassium and renal function.
  • SGLT2 Inhibitors (Dapagliflozin, Empagliflozin): Originally diabetes drugs, now foundational for HF regardless of diabetes status. Reduce hospitalizations and mortality.

Diuretics (e.g., Furosemide) are used strictly for symptom relief (fluid overload) but do not confer a mortality benefit. They remain important for managing congestion.

High-Yield All four pillars should be initiated simultaneously (or as rapidly as tolerated) rather than sequentially. Delaying uptitration of each class one after the other is an outdated approach. The key principle is "start low, go slow, but start all four."

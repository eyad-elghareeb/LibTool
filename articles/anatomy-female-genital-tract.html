Anatomy of the Female Genital Tract

A comprehensive guide to the external and internal female reproductive organs, including histology, blood supply, and clinical surgical anatomy.

External Genitalia (Vulva)

The external genitalia, collectively known as the vulva, includes the mons veneris, labia majora, labia minora, clitoris, and the vestibule.

  • Mons veneris: A pad of fat located over the symphysis pubis, covered by hairy skin.

Bartholin's Glands

Also known as the "Greater Vestibular Glands," these are bilateral, pea-sized (1 cm) compound racemose glands lined by cuboidal to columnar epithelium.

  • Site: Situated deep in the posterior 1/3 of the labia majora, in contact with the posterior vestibular bulbs.
  • Ducts: Approximately 2 cm long, opening into the groove between the hymen and labia minus at the 5 and 7 o'clock positions.
  • Function: Secrete thin, mucoid, alkaline fluid during sexual excitement acting as a lubricant.
High-YieldBartholin's glands are usually not palpable unless diseased. A swelling in the posterior 1/3 of the labia majora is typically a Bartholin's cyst or abscess. Standard treatment for a cyst is marsupialization.

Perineum, Vestibule, and Hymen

The Perineum

The perineum lies between the vaginal orifice anteriorly and the anus posteriorly. It consists of the perineal body, a fibromuscular tendon supported by several muscles:

  • Superficial and deep transverse perinei muscles.
  • Bulbospongiosus muscle.
  • Deep fibers of the levator ani (pubococcygeus part).
Clinical WarningThe ischiocavernosus muscle does NOT share in the formation of the perineal body.

The Vestibule and Hymen

The vestibule is the space between the labia minora containing the openings for the urethra, vagina, and Bartholin's ducts. The hymen is a mucous membrane closing the lower vaginal orifice in virgins.

  • Imperforate hymen: Leads to cryptomenorrhea (hidden menstruation).
  • Carunculae myrtiformis: Remnants of the hymen after vaginal delivery.
  • Fossa navicularis: The depression between the hymen and the fourchette, typically only seen in virgins.

The Uterus: Anatomy and Axis

The uterus is an inverted pear-shaped, muscular organ. In a standing position, the external os is at the level of the ischial spine, while the internal os is at the level of the symphysis pubis.

  • Size: Approximately 1 x 2 x 3 inches.
  • Weight: 50–80 grams.
  • Normal Position: Anteverted and Anteflexed (AVF).
TermDefinition
AnteversionThe cervix is bent forward on the axis of the vagina at a right angle (90°).
AnteflexionThe body of the uterus is bent forward on the axis of the cervix at an obtuse angle (150–160°).

Histology and Blood Supply

Histology of the Uterine Body

  • Endometrium: Tubular/columnar epithelium. Differentiates into the superficial compact layer (shed), middle spongy layer (shed), and basal compact layer (responsible for regeneration).
  • Myometrium: Three layers of muscle. The intermediate layer contains interlacing fibers ("living ligatures") that compress blood vessels to control blood loss after delivery.
  • Perimetrium: Peritoneal covering. Forms the vesicouterine pouch anteriorly and the Douglas pouch (rectouterine pouch) posteriorly.

Blood Supply

The primary supply is the uterine artery, a branch of the anterior division of the internal iliac artery. It is highly tortuous and crosses 1.5 cm lateral to the cervix.

High-Yield: Water Under the BridgeThe uterine artery crosses SUPERIOR to the ureter. This is a critical landmark during hysterectomy to avoid accidental ureteric ligation.

The Fallopian Tubes

The tubes are 10–12 cm long and divided into four parts (medial to lateral):

  • Interstitial part: Inserted into the uterine wall.
  • Isthmus: The narrowest extra-uterine part.
  • Ampulla: The widest and longest part (5–6 cm); the site of fertilization.
  • Infundibulum: Fimbriated end; the "fimbria ovarica" is attached to the ovary to help pick up the ovum.

Histology: The endosalpinx contains ciliated cells (move cilia toward the uterus), goblet cells (secretory), and peg cells (reserve cells).

The Ovaries

The ovaries are the primary sex organs, located in the ovarian fossa on the lateral pelvic wall. They are retroperitoneal and measure approximately 1 x 2 x 3 cm.

  • Anatomy: Consists of a hilum (vessel entry), cortex (contains follicles), and medulla (fibrous core).
  • Arterial Supply: Ovarian artery arises directly from the aorta.
  • Venous Drainage: The right ovarian vein drains to the IVC, while the left ovarian vein drains to the left renal vein.

Ligamentous Supports and the Pelvic Ureter

Ligamentous Supports

  • Cardinal Ligaments (Mackenrodt's): The main support of the uterus; fan-shaped pelvic fascia.
  • Uterosacral Ligaments: Maintain the anteversion position.
  • Broad Ligament: A double sheet of peritoneum containing the tubes, round ligament, ovarian ligament, vessels, and vestigial remnants (e.g., Gartner's duct).

The Pelvic Ureter

The ureter is a 25 cm muscular tube. In the pelvis, it crosses the common iliac artery and runs 1.5 cm lateral to the supra-vaginal cervix before entering the bladder.

Clinical Warning: Ureteric InjuryPoints of high risk for ureteric injury during gynaecologic surgery include: ligation of the infundibulo-pelvic ligament, ligation of uterine vessels, and during the dissection of the cardinal ligament tunnel.

Acute Appendicitis

Acute inflammation of the vermiform appendix — one of the most common causes of acute abdomen requiring emergency surgery.

Clinical Presentation

Classic presentation begins with vague periumbilical pain that migrates to the right lower quadrant (RLQ) over 12–24 hours. This migration of pain is the most reliable historical feature and occurs as inflammation progresses from the visceral peritoneum to the parietal peritoneum.

  • Anorexia: Present in nearly all cases; its absence should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis. This is a distinguishing feature from other causes of abdominal pain.
  • Nausea and vomiting: Follow the onset of pain (not before). Vomiting preceding pain is atypical for appendicitis.
  • McBurney point tenderness: Maximum tenderness at one-third the distance from the anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS) to the umbilicus.
  • Rovsing sign: RLQ pain with palpation of the left lower quadrant (LLQ), indicating localized peritoneal irritation.
  • Psoas sign: RLQ pain with hip extension (retrocecal appendix inflaming the psoas muscle).
  • Obturator sign: RLQ pain with internal hip rotation (pelvic appendix inflaming the obturator internus).
High-Yield Vomiting that precedes pain is atypical for appendicitis and should suggest gastroenteritis or other diagnoses. The sequence of symptoms — periumbilical pain first, then migration to RLQ, then anorexia/nausea — is highly characteristic.

Diagnosis & Imaging

Diagnosis is primarily clinical, supported by laboratory tests and imaging when the diagnosis is uncertain. The goal is to minimize delays while avoiding negative appendectomy rates:

  • CT scan (abdomen/pelvis with contrast): Sensitivity ~94%, specificity ~95%. Gold standard for adults. Look for appendiceal dilation (>6 mm), wall enhancement, periappendiceal fat stranding, and appendicolith.
  • Ultrasound: First-line in children, pregnant women, and young females. Non-compressible appendix >6 mm is diagnostic. Operator-dependent; may be inconclusive in obese patients or those with significant bowel gas.
  • Lab: Leukocytosis (WBC 10–18K), mild left shift. CRP often elevated. A normal WBC does not exclude appendicitis — clinical correlation is essential.
Clinical Warning In pregnant patients, the appendix migrates superiorly as gestation progresses due to the enlarging uterus. In the third trimester, RLQ pain may actually present in the right upper quadrant. Always adjust your examination expectations and consider MRI if ultrasound is inconclusive during pregnancy.

Treatment

Appendectomy remains the definitive treatment for acute appendicitis. The approach depends on the severity and presence of complications:

  • Laparoscopic appendectomy: Standard of care for uncomplicated cases. Shorter recovery, lower wound infection rate, better cosmesis, and earlier return to normal activity compared to open surgery. Convert to open if perforation with generalized peritonitis is encountered.
  • Antibiotics-first approach: Selected uncomplicated cases may be managed with IV antibiotics alone (e.g., ertapenem or ceftriaxone + metronidazole), but recurrence rate is ~25% at 1 year. This approach is gaining acceptance but is not yet standard for most patients.
  • Perforated appendicitis: Requires broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone + metronidazole or piperacillin/tazobactam), aggressive fluid resuscitation, and urgent surgical consultation. An abscess may be drained percutaneously first, followed by interval appendectomy ~6–8 weeks later once inflammation resolves.

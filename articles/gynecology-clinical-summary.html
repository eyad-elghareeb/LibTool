Manual of Gynecology: Comprehensive Clinical Summary

High-yield gynecology concepts covering anatomy, menstrual disorders, PCOS, AUB, fibroids, endometriosis, infertility, and contraception with diagnostic flowcharts and treatment algorithms.

1. Anatomy Quick Reference

Surgical Pearl: Uterine artery crosses ABOVE the ureter 1.5 cm lateral to the supra-vaginal cervix (water flows under the bridge). Critical in hysterectomy to avoid ureteric injury.

Ureteric Injury - High-Yield List

Points of ureteric injury during surgery: (1) At infundibulo-pelvic ligament, (2) Lateral pelvic wall above uterosacral ligament, (3) Beneath uterine vessels, (4) Tunnel in cardinal ligament, (5) Intramural portion, (6) Devascularization of lower 1/3.

2. Puberty and Menopause

Puberty

Timing: 9-14 years. Earliest sign: Growth spurt. Sequence (TAGS-M): Thelarche (breast) then Adrenarche (pubic/axillary hair) then Growth spurt then Sexual maturity then Menarche. Tanner staging uses Stages 1-5 for breast (thelarche) and pubic hair (adrenarche). Menarche is the first spontaneous menstruation.

Menopause

Definition: Permanent cessation of menstruation for more than 12 months due to complete follicular depletion. Mean age: 51 years (range 45-55). Premature menopause: Before age 40. Diagnostic hormone level: FSH greater than 40 IU (persistent).

Hormonal changes: Increased FSH, LH increased 3x, decreased E2 (main estrogen shifts to E1), decreased progesterone, relatively increased androgens. Major symptoms: Hot flushes and osteoporosis are the MAJOR symptoms of menopausal syndrome.

Key Regimen: Best HRT regimen is cyclic combined estrogen plus progesterone. Alternative non-bleeding agent: Tibolone. Phytoestrogen: Klima Dynon.

Premenstrual Tension Syndrome (PMS/PMDD)

Definition: Physical, psychological, and behavioral changes in the late luteal phase, improved at onset of flow. Main symptoms: Carbohydrate craving, salt and water retention (edema), anxiety, depression.

Physical: Facial/peripheral edema, breast tension, abdominal bloating, headache. Psychological: Depression, anxiety, irritability, sleep disturbance, suicidal ideation, criminal behaviour.

Treatment: Psychotherapy, diuretics, COCs, Bromocriptine, serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), Vitamin B complex, primrose oil.

PMS Management Flowchart

PRESENTATION: Late luteal phase symptoms recurring monthly with physical and psychological features. First, EXCLUDE ORGANIC CAUSE with hormonal workup (prolactin, TSH, E2/P ratio) and symptom diary for at least 2 cycles.

MILD-MODERATE: Psychotherapy, exercise, dietary modification (low salt/caffeine), Vitamin B6, primrose oil.

MODERATE-SEVERE: SSRIs (1st line), COCs, Diuretics (spironolactone), Bromocriptine for mastalgia.

REFRACTORY: GnRH analogue with add-back HRT; rarely bilateral oophorectomy.

4. Amenorrhea

Golden Rule: PREGNANCY is the COMMONEST cause of secondary amenorrhea. Always do beta-hCG FIRST!

Amenorrhea Diagnostic Flowchart

STEP 1 - EXCLUDE PREGNANCY: Beta-hCG. If positive, manage as pregnancy.

STEP 2 - LOCAL EXAM: Exclude outflow obstruction: imperforate hymen, transverse septum, cervical stenosis.

STEP 3 - ASSESS SECONDARY SEX CHARS AND UTERUS: Use the breast present/absent multiplied by uterus present/absent table for different diagnoses.

STEP 4 - PROLACTIN LEVEL: If elevated prolactin, check TSH (hypothyroidism?) then brain CT/MRI to exclude pituitary adenoma.

STEP 5 - PROGESTERONE WITHDRAWAL TEST: Positive bleeding indicates ovarian/luteal phase defect. Negative: give E+P. If positive then central cause. If negative then uterine/end-organ failure.

STEP 6 - FSH LEVEL: Elevated FSH indicates gonadal failure. Low FSH: GnRH test. If FSH rises then hypothalamic failure; if not then pituitary failure.

Asherman's Syndrome

Definition: Intrauterine adhesions between anterior and posterior walls. Cause: Excessive curettage (most common), CS, myomectomy, post-inflammatory. Presentation: Amenorrhea/oligo-hypomenorrhea, infertility, recurrent abortion. Diagnosis: HSG (filling defects) plus Hysteroscopy (gold standard). Treatment: Hysteroscopic adhesiolysis then IUD for 2 cycles then Estrogen 3-4 weeks.

5. Hyperprolactinaemia

Definition: Prolactin greater than 20 ng/dL.

Causes (P-D-E-L-T-S-H Mnemonic)

Physiological (pregnancy, lactation, stress, sleep, 2nd half cycle). Drugs (antihypertensives, antipsychotics, H2-blockers, opioids). Ectopic tumor release. Local breast lesion. Tumors (pituitary prolactinoma). Syndromes (Chiari-Frommel, Del Castillo). Hypothyroidism.

Clinical Features

Presentation: Galactorrhea plus amenorrhea (galactorrhea-amenorrhea syndrome), infertility, headache, visual field defects. Investigation: Serum prolactin, TSH, Brain CT/MRI. Medical Treatment: Dopamine agonist: Bromocriptine (Parlodel) or Cabergoline (preferred - fewer side effects). Surgical/RT: Micro/macroadenoma not responsive to medical therapy.

Associations: Galactorrhea plus Amenorrhea syndromes: (1) Lactational, (2) Chiari-Frommel, (3) Del Castillo, (4) Pituitary tumors, (5) Drugs (antidepressants). Amenorrhea plus Hirsutism: (1) PCOS, (2) Virilizing ovarian tumors.

6. Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome (PCOS)

Rotterdam Criteria (2 of 3 required): (1) Oligo/anovulation, (2) Clinical/biochemical hyperandrogenism, (3) PCO morphology on USS (12+ follicles 2-9 mm OR volume greater than 10 cm3 in ONE ovary).

Pathophysiology (Vicious Cycle)

Starting point: INSULIN RESISTANCE plus HYPERINSULINEMIA from genetic/dietary predisposition. This leads to INCREASED OVARIAN ANDROGEN PRODUCTION as theca cells become overactivated by insulin and LH. Peripheral conversion of androgens to E1 occurs in adipose tissue via aromatization, creating a hyperestrogenic state. This causes FSH SUPPRESSION and LH STIMULATION (low FSH causes follicular arrest; high LH causes more androgen production), perpetuating the vicious cycle and resulting in CHRONIC ANOVULATION with multiple arrested follicles giving the polycystic appearance on USS.

Normal Limits (FIGO)

Frequency: 24-38 days. Duration: Up to 8 days. Volume: Normal approximately 30-80 mL; Heavy is greater than 80 mL. Regularity: Variation shortest to longest is 9 days or less.

AUB Management Algorithm

ACUTE AUB - Goal: Stop current bleeding. Medical options include IV Conjugated estrogen 25 mg q4-6h for 24 hours, COCs (35 mcg EE 3x/day for 7 days), MPA 20 mg 3x/day for 7 days, or Tranexamic acid 1.3 g 3x/day for 5 days.

CHRONIC AUB - Goal: Reduce future blood loss and treat underlying cause. Medical: Continue acute regimens, LNG-IUD (Mirena), Etonogestrel implant. Surgical: Hysteroscopic myomectomy/polypectomy, Endometrial ablation, Myomectomy (lap/open), Hysterectomy.

D&C / ENDOMETRIAL BIOPSY: Required in MOST cases for histopathological diagnosis of endometrial pathology.

Coagulopathy Screening: Screen for coagulopathy if: (1) Heavy bleeding since menarche, (2) Postpartum/surgical bleeding, (3) At least 2 of: bruising, epistaxis, gum bleeding, family history. Tests: PT, PTT, APTT, fibrinogen, vWF antigen, Factor VIII.

8. Uterine Leiomyoma (Fibroid)

Epidemiology: MOST COMMON uterine tumor; affects 20-30% of women over 30 years. Estrogen-dependent. Common in nullipara, low parity, black race. Does NOT appear de novo after menopause.

Red Degeneration Rule: Red degeneration in pregnancy - NEVER operate acutely! Treat conservatively (bed rest plus analgesics). Surgery only if conservative treatment fails, then myomectomy for affected tumors only.

Fibroid Management Flowchart

When fibroid is diagnosed clinically plus USS confirmation: if ASYMPTOMATIC, no treatment if small, BUT treat if greater than 12 weeks size, rapid growth, pedunculated sub-serous, submucosal in nullipara, or large cervical. If NEAR MENOPAUSE with small size and mild symptoms, use conservative medical treatment with GnRH analogue or progestogens for 6-9 months. If YOUNG (under 40) and desires fertility, perform myomectomy (abdominal/laparoscopic/hysteroscopic based on location). If over 40 or family complete or multiple fibroids or malignancy suspected, perform hysterectomy (total preferred; vaginal/laparoscopic/open).

9. Adenomyosis

Definition: Endometrial glands and stroma WITHIN the myometrium with adjacent smooth muscle hyperplasia.

Key Features

Incidence: Approximately 15%; middle-aged, high parity, white race. Etiology (most common theory): Invagination / Cullen's diverticulum theory. Gross appearance: Symmetrical (diffuse) or asymmetrical (localized) enlargement less than 10 weeks; blood spots on cut section; NO capsule, NO whorl.

Classic symptoms: AUB-A (HMB) plus CRESCENDO secondary dysmenorrhea. Signs: Uterus less than 10 weeks, tender on bimanual. USS criteria: Myometrial cysts, sub-endometrial nodules, asymmetric thickening, poor endo-myometrial border definition. MRI (most accurate): Junctional zone 8-12 mm is suspicious; 12 mm or greater is diagnostic. Histological diagnosis: Mostly from hysterectomy specimen.

Adenomyosis Management

When adenomyosis is suspected (AUB plus crescendo dysmenorrhea plus tender uterus less than 10 weeks), perform USS as primary screening looking for JZ thickening and myometrial cysts. If USS is equivocal, use MRI. JZ 12 mm or greater confirms the diagnosis. For MEDICAL treatment (minimal disease or near menopause): NSAIDs, COCs, high-dose progestogens, or LNG-IUS (Mirena). For SURGICAL treatment (definitive): Hysterectomy without oophorectomy unless specific indication.

10. Endometriosis

Key Facts: Definition: Endometrial tissue (glands plus stroma) OUTSIDE the uterine cavity. Most common site: OVARIES (65%) causing chocolate cysts. LAPAROSCOPY is the gold standard for diagnosis. Known as the disease of PAIN plus INFERTILITY.

Etiology and Predisposing Factors

Most accepted etiology: Implantation/transplantation (retrograde menstruation theory). Other theories: Celomic metaplasia, vascular-lymphatic spread, genetic (7x risk if 1st-degree relative), immunologic. Predisposing factors: Uterine manipulation during menstruation (HSG, IUCD insertion), outflow obstruction.

Pelvic sites: Ovaries (most common), cul-de-sac, uterosacral ligaments, rectovaginal septum, bladder, bowel. Extra-pelvic sites: Umbilicus, episiotomy scars, lung (catamenial pneumothorax), nasal mucosa. Microscopic diagnosis: Endometrial glands plus stroma plus hemorrhage (all 3 needed). Lab marker: CA-125 (elevated; non-specific).

Symptoms

Pelvic pain (chronic more than 6 months): diffuse or localized; due to prostaglandins, histamine, peritoneal scarring. Infertility (most common complication): severe cases cause mechanical obstruction from adhesions; mild cases cause anovulation, LUF syndrome, altered tubal motility, endometrial defect. Crescendo dysmenorrhea: pain worsens as cycle progresses. Dyspareunia: deep, due to involvement of uterosacral ligaments and rectovaginal septum. Dysuria plus cyclic hematuria indicates bladder endometriosis. Dyschazia plus hematochezia indicates bowel involvement.

Endometriosis Management Algorithm

When endometriosis is suspected (chronic pelvic pain plus dysmenorrhea plus infertility plus dyspareunia), order CA-125 and USS/MRI. If CA-125 is elevated with chocolate cysts on USS or adhesions on MRI, proceed to LAPAROSCOPY (gold standard) to visualize implants, chocolate cysts, and adhesions with biopsy for histology.

For MILD / YOUNG / NEAR MENOPAUSE: Expectant management with NSAIDs, COCs (non-stop), Gestagens (Provera), GnRH analogue (medical menopause), or Oral GnRH antagonist (elagolix/relugolix). For YOUNG patients NEEDING FERTILITY: Conservative surgery with adhesiolysis, excision of endometriomas, LUNA, surface reconstruction. For SEVERE / RECURRENT / FAMILY COMPLETE: Total hysterectomy plus bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy (abdominal/laparoscopic/robotic).

11. Endometrial Hyperplasia (EH)

High Yield: EH is the PRECURSOR of endometrial cancer (most common gynecological malignancy in Western world). Caused by PROLONGED UNOPPOSED ESTROGEN. Atypical EH has 28% risk of cancer over 20 years.

2014 WHO Classification

TypeArchitectureCytologyCancer Risk (20 y)Treatment
EH without atypiaSimple compact glands; dense stromaNormal cellsLess than 5%Progestins / LNG-IUS
Atypical EHCrowded glands; increased mitosesAtypical cells28%Hysterectomy (or high-dose progestin if fertility desired)

Risk Factors

  • Obesity: increased peripheral androgen-to-estrogen conversion in adipose tissue
  • Anovulation: perimenopause, PCOS
  • Estrogen-secreting ovarian tumors: e.g., granulosa cell tumor (up to 40% EH prevalence)
  • Drugs: systemic ERT (unopposed), long-term tamoxifen

EH Management Flowchart

When AUB occurs in peri/post-menopausal woman (or AUB-M), suspect EH/cancer. Perform investigations: TVS (endometrial thickness) then Endometrial biopsy/D&C then Hysteroscopy.

For EH WITHOUT ATYPIA: Progestins 10-20 mg/day for 6 months OR LNG-IUS for 5 years, with follow-up biopsy every 6-12 months.

For ATYPICAL EH in reproductive age (desires fertility): High-dose progestins plus LNG-IUS, biopsy every 3-6 months, hysterectomy if no regression.

For ATYPICAL EH with completed family or postmenopausal: HYSTERECTOMY (vaginal/laparoscopic preferred) plus bilateral salpingectomy.

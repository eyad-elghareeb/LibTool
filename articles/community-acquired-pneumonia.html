Community-Acquired Pneumonia

An acute infection of the pulmonary parenchyma acquired outside of a healthcare setting.

Overview

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) is a common, potentially lethal infection of the lung parenchyma. It presents with fever, cough, sputum production, pleuritic chest pain, and dyspnea. CAP is a leading cause of hospitalization and infectious disease mortality worldwide, particularly affecting the elderly, immunocompromised, and those with comorbid conditions.

High-Yield Streptococcus pneumoniae remains the most common typical bacterial cause of CAP globally. Always consider atypical pathogens (Mycoplasma, Legionella, Chlamydia) in the differential, especially in younger patients or those with specific risk factors.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis requires clinical features (fever, cough, dyspnea) plus an infiltrate on chest imaging. The workup should aim to identify the causative organism when possible and assess severity:

  • Chest X-ray: Lobar consolidation suggests typical bacterial (e.g., S. pneumoniae); interstitial infiltrates suggest atypical (e.g., Mycoplasma).
  • Labs: CBC (leukocytosis with left shift), BMP (electrolytes, renal function), LFTs, lactate, procalcitonin (useful for guiding antibiotic duration).
  • Sputum Gram stain & culture: Recommended in hospitalized patients, especially if severe or not responding to initial therapy.
  • Urinary antigens: S. pneumoniae and Legionella antigen testing should be ordered in severe cases or when these pathogens are suspected.
  • Blood cultures: Should be drawn before antibiotics in severe CAP requiring ICU admission; positive in only ~5–10% of cases.

Severity Assessment

Site-of-care decisions (outpatient vs. inpatient vs. ICU) rely on validated scoring systems that estimate mortality risk:

ToolUseKey Parameters
CURB-65Quick bedside assessmentConfusion, Uremia (BUN >19), RR ≥ 30, BP low (SBP <90 or DBP ≤60), Age ≥ 65
PSI / PORTMortality estimate20 variables, 5 risk classes (I–V). More comprehensive but less practical at bedside.
Clinical Warning CURB-65 score ≥ 3 mandates hospital admission. Score ≥ 5 warrants ICU consideration. Do not rely on a single score alone — always integrate clinical judgment, patient comorbidities, social factors, and overall clinical appearance.

Treatment

Empiric antibiotic therapy should be initiated promptly, guided by severity and local resistance patterns. Treatment is stratified by site of care:

Outpatient (Healthy, No Comorbidities)

  • Amoxicillin 1g TID or Doxycycline 100mg BID or Macrolide (Azithromycin 500mg day 1, then 250mg daily × 4 days)

Outpatient (With Comorbidities)

  • Amoxicillin/clavulanate 875/125mg BID + Macrolide or Respiratory fluoroquinolone (Levofloxacin 750mg daily)

Inpatient (Non-ICU)

  • Beta-lactam (Ceftriaxone 1g IV daily, Cefotaxime) + Macrolide or Respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy
High-Yield Antibiotics should be administered within 4 hours of hospital arrival for CAP. Delaying antibiotics beyond this window is associated with increased mortality. Procalcitonin-guided therapy can help shorten antibiotic duration in mild-to-moderate cases.
